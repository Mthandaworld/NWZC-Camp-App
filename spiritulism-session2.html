SESSION 2: PHILOSOPHICAL AND CULTURAL FOUNDATIONS OF SPIRITUALISM

 

As is common in all fallen cultures of mankind, historical records usually represent the life of the majority, and the minority often go unrecorded. Cultural practices (right and wrong) have been inherited from generation to generation over many years, until it became part of life. Many people who are in support of spiritualism attribute it to inherited culture, and these belief stays with them as far as their ignorance of truth lasts.

 

Immortality of the soul means that the soul is immortal, that only the body dies and decomposes, but there is an inner person or soul that does not die, but continues to live in the spiritual realm after a person dies. Ancient pagan philosophies viewed the soul as immortal and death as the beginning of the next phase of existence. The philosophers applied the concept of dualism in explaining the nature of man. They described a human being as made up of two distinct substances, the body (temporary) and the soul (immortal). At death, the immortal soul is liberated from the body to enter into the afterlife, where it will continue to live and through a process of purification unto perfection.

John Mbiti, in his book, The African Religions and Philosophy describes the African cultural view of spirits as follows, “The spirits in general belong to the ontological mode of existence between God and man. Broadly speaking, we can recognize two categories of spiritual beings: those which were created as such, and those which were once human beings.” suggesting that dead become part of the spirits that populate the spirit world.

 

It must be a myth to assume that before the coming of the bible, the spirits of the dead played a mediatory role between God and people. In biblical terms, what is false today has never been true at any time in the past. However deep the ignorance was, God could never at any time collaborate with Satan to bless or communicate with His people. God has always attended to people’s needs (e.g. rain, sun), because they are His, despite their goodness or wickedness (Matthew 5:45). 

The acceptance of this immortality of the soul belief amongst Christians and many cultures has given room to many false assumptions including the following:

·         The saints enter heaven at the point of death

·         The wicked go straight to hell at death

·         The wicked are tormented eternally in the fires of hell

·         The wicked dead can be cleansed in purgatory before going to heaven

·         Dead saints enter heaven at the point of death

·         Prayers can be offered to God through the dead saints

·         The living can interact with the dead people

·         The dead can visit the living through spirit mediums

·         The dead can take care of the living or minister to their needs

·         The dead can harm the living as a way of revenge or by virtue of their wickedness

·         The dead can appear in person or in other forms to show their power

 

Historically, such beliefs crept into the church during the years of apostasy, after the rise of the papacy in 538 A.D, and before the reformation. The church, which had formerly upheld the pure doctrines of the bible, now embraced human traditions and superstitions above the bible. “The theory of the immortality of the soul was one of those false doctrines that Rome, borrowing from paganism, incorporated into the religion of Christendom.” E. G. White, The Great Controversy, p. 551.
